Defense Protocol — 5 Tiers

How to
Protect
Yourself

A layered defense framework. Start at Tier 1 and work upward — every tier completed meaningfully reduces your attack surface.

Tools like WiFi Pineapple, Rubber Ducky, O.MG Cables, LAN Turtle and Shark Jack are commonly used to hijack networks, steal credentials, and execute malicious commands through everday-looking hardware. Your best defenses are simple: never plug in unknown USB devices or cables, turn off auto-connect on you devices, use a VPN on public networks, moniter your network traffic, and keep your firmware up to date. Physical Security Matters just as much as digital. If something is plugged into your network that shouldn't be there, the damage is already done.

Good security doesn't require being an expert — it requires layers. This list is organized from the most accessible and impactful steps (Tier 1) through advanced technical controls (Tier 5). Most successful attacks exploit Tier 1 failures against people who assumed they were beyond needing the basics. Start there and work up.

Protection Tier List

T1
Basic
Strong Passwords
Use unique passwords of 12+ characters for every account. Never reuse — one breach exposes everything.
Enable MFA Everywhere
Turn on multi-factor authentication wherever it's available — especially email, banking, and social accounts.
Keep Software Updated
Apply OS and app updates promptly. Most real attacks exploit known vulnerabilities with patches already available.
Verify Links First
Hover over links to see the real URL before clicking. When in doubt, navigate directly to the site.
Only Your Own Cables
Never plug in USB cables you didn't purchase yourself. Avoid public charging stations without a data blocker.
T2
Smart
Password Manager
Use Bitwarden or 1Password to generate and store strong unique passwords automatically — no memorization needed.
Authenticator App MFA
Upgrade from SMS 2FA to an authenticator app. SMS codes are vulnerable to SIM swapping attacks.
VPN on Public WiFi
Always use a reputable VPN on public or untrusted networks to encrypt traffic against interception.
Verify Networks First
Ask staff for the official SSID before connecting. Look for duplicate network names. Avoid open networks entirely.
USB Data Blockers
Use a USB data blocker at public charging ports — allows power, blocks all data transfer completely.
T3
Hardened
Hardware Security Keys
Use a FIDO2 key (YubiKey) as primary MFA — completely immune to phishing and SIM swapping.
Email Alias Strategy
Use SimpleLogin so breaches at one site don't expose your real email address anywhere else.
WPA3 & Disable WPS
Upgrade your router to WPA3. Disable WPS immediately — it has been cryptographically broken.
Full Disk Encryption
Enable BitLocker or FileVault so data is unreadable if your device is physically stolen or imaged.
Encrypted DNS
Use DNS over HTTPS via Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 to prevent DNS-based traffic analysis and hijacking.
Disable USB Auto-run
Configure your OS to never auto-execute code from USB devices. Restrict HID where not required.
T4
Advanced
Network Segmentation
Separate IoT, guest access, and work machines onto different VLANs so a compromise can't spread laterally.
Intrusion Detection
Deploy Zeek, Suricata, or a WIPS to alert on deauth attacks, rogue APs, and ARP spoofing events.
Canary Tokens
Plant honeytoken files and fake credentials that alert you if accessed — early warning of a breach.
Certificate Transparency
Monitor CT logs for certificates issued for your domains — early sign of phishing infrastructure.
RFID Shielding
Use RFID-blocking wallets to prevent contactless skimming of credit cards and access badges in public.
T5
Expert
Air-Gapped Systems
Isolate the most sensitive work on machines with no network connection. Transfer only via verified media.
Verified Boot
Use hardware with verified boot chains (Pixel, iOS) and secure enclaves for cryptographic key storage.
Firmware Auditing
Audit router and peripheral firmware. Consider open-source alternatives like OpenWrt to remove vendor backdoors.
Formal Threat Modeling
Document your assets and adversaries. Regularly red-team your setup using the tools covered on the Devices page.
Zero Trust Architecture
Treat every device as untrusted by default. Enforce least-privilege access, microsegmentation, and continuous verification.
